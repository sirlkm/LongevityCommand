Section 04
Longevity Credit System — The Ethical Data Exchange

📥 How Credits Accrue

Every dollar of pharma data licensing revenue attributable to a member's anonymized data contribution generates credits. 30% of gross pharma revenue is pooled and distributed proportionally to the contributing members.

Monthly Credit = (Member Data Value / Total Pool Value) × 30% Pharma Revenue

📊 Example at Scale (Year 3)

Illustrative model at 75,000 members. Pharma licensing revenue is subject to market validation — actual pricing per cohort study TBD through partner conversations:

$50M–$150M pharma revenue × 30% = $15M–$45M member credit pool

At midpoint ($100M), loyalty multiplier (10-yr): 1.5× → $600–$900/yr back to long-tenured members. Average annual premium: ~$2,400. Net member cost after credits: $1,500–$1,800. Figures improve significantly as pharma partnership pricing is confirmed.

💳 3 Redemption Options

Option A — Premium Reduction
Apply credits to reduce next year's premium. Up to 80% reduction cap. Most popular election.

Option B — HSA Contribution
Direct deposit to member's Health Savings Account. Tax-advantaged. Capped at IRS HSA limit.

Option C — Cash Payout
Annual check or direct deposit. Taxable income. No cap. Preferred by retirees and near-retirement members.

Competitive Moat — What We Understand That Others Don't
vs. Traditional Life / AD&D Insurers

They see first responders as high-risk liabilities

Incumbents price first responders out of the market or exclude cardiac from occupational coverage. They have no data intelligence layer — just actuarial tables.

We use AI to predict and prevent the cardiac events that make this population "uninsurable" — turning the risk premium into an opportunity.
vs. Wearable / Digital Health Companies

They collect data but can't monetize it ethically

Apple Health, Garmin, Whoop — they have device data but no distribution into first responder benefits. They also have no ethical monetization mechanism that returns value to the data contributor.

We've solved the ethical data extraction problem. The credit system transforms surveillance into a cooperative economic model.
vs. Pharma / Clinical Research Platforms

They can't access first responder cardiac data at scale

Pharma needs longitudinal data from high-stress, high-cardiac-risk occupational cohorts. This doesn't exist anywhere. IRB recruiting for this population takes years and millions.

We're building the only consented, longitudinal first responder cardiac data asset in existence. By Year 3, it's irreplaceable.
vs. General Health Insurance Platforms

They have no occupational intelligence

Oscar, Collective Health, etc. are optimizing for general population health management. None have built for the unique physiology, psychology, and occupational hazards of first responders.

Our domain expertise and union distribution are 5-year moats that generalist platforms cannot replicate without our data.
vs. Workers' Comp Carriers

They respond to events; we prevent them

Workers' comp is reactive — claims management after injury or death. There is no prevention layer, no predictive intelligence, and no financial incentive to keep workers healthy before an event.

We're building the prevention layer that reduces workers' comp claims — making us a natural partner or acquisition target for these carriers.
vs. AI Health Startups

They lack the insurance distribution wedge

Most AI health startups are selling SaaS to hospital systems. They face long sales cycles and have no recurring revenue flywheel. Without an insurance product, they can't capture the full value chain.

Insurance is our distribution weapon. Every enrolled member is a captive, long-term data contributor. SaaS startups have no equivalent lock-in mechanism.
